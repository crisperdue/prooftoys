Logic through Pictures

True statements

Mathematics is especially interested in statements that are true, particularly statements that are true by mathematical reasoning, in other words theorems.

As an example let's consider the statement that every number is either less than 100 or greater than 10. We could state this as x < 100 or x > 10. In the picture for this statement all shaded areas represent cases where the statement is true.

Implication -- "implies"

Let's look at another typical kind of statement that has a logical meaning, such as "all birds have wings". We might also express this as "if x is a bird then x has wings". In our notation we can write (bird x) implies (wings x) or alternatively bird x --> wings x.

The picture below illustrates a scenario where the statement is true. The area representing all things with wings entirely encloses the area that represents all birds. (In this picture there are also some things with wings that are not birds, such as bats in the real world.)

Any picture of this kind where one area encloses another (or occupies the same area) illustrates a true statement of this kind. But is there a system for shading pictures like this so they are completely shaded exactly when the statement is true? It turns out there is.

It is clear right away which part of the picture makes the statement not always true -- the part of the "birds" circle outside the "wings" area.

Anything in the picture that is not a bird does not affect the truth of the statement. If something is not a bird, it does not affect whether this statement about birds is true. So we have found that it is possible to define a function implies (alternatively -->) that enables us to make statements that say one thing implies another. It works like and and or, taking two boolean values as inputs and producing a boolean (true or false) result.

Vacuous statements

Some peculiar situations lead to results that may seem a bit strange. What if we say that all birds larger than elephants can fly in outer space? If there are no birds larger than elephants in our world, in mathematics this is considered a true statement! The reason it is considered true is that indeed there are no examples that break the rule, that is no birds that are larger than elephants and cannot fly in outer space!

In situations where there is nothing with a property A, it is true to say that A implies anything at all, no matter how silly or impossible. Mathematicians recognize that this is a strange situation and refer to such as statements as vacuously true, meaning true but useless because there can never be a counterexample and it doesn't matter what the conclusion of the statement is.
